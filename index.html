Designing a Minimal Resilient Mesh for Wood County, WV

A data story about emergency readiness, difficult terrain, and network resilience. Scroll to see how elevation, line-of-sight, and population density shape a practical Meshtastic deployment plan in Appalachia.

Scroll
Section 1

The Problem

When commercial cellular and the county's public-safety trunked radio fail simultaneously - flood, ice storm, prolonged grid outage - Wood County has no fallback messaging plane. The goal: a volunteer-deployable 915 MHz LoRa mesh that carries short text and telemetry between fire stations, EOCs, hospitals, and amateur operators independent of any external infrastructure.

Section 2

Why Terrain Decides Everything

At 915 MHz, LoRa is effectively a line-of-sight medium. Wood County ranges from the 180 m Ohio River floodplain up to 400 m ridges, with deeply incised tributaries. A node in a valley sees nothing; a node on a ridge sees tens of kilometers. Siting is the only free variable that matters; power, antenna, and firmware are commodity.

Section 3

What We're Covering

Coverage is scored against Microsoft Building Footprints - 46,406 structures in the county. That proxies households and workplaces far better than census tracts. A site's value is the count of buildings within its 14.3 km radio horizon, weighted 85% buildings / 15% topographic prominence.

Section 4

Link Budget

Friis at 915 MHz with a Meshtastic-class radio: Pt 20 dBm, Gt 5 dBi, Gr 2 dBi, Pr sens −137 dBm, 10 dB fade margin. That closes a clear-air link to ~200 km - but the Earth curves and the terrain doesn't care about your math.

Section 5

Fresnel & the 4/3 Earth

Every candidate pair is traced along a DEM-sampled great-circle path with 4/3-Earth curvature correction. A link only counts if 60% of the first Fresnel zone is clear along the entire profile - endpoint-only LoS checks overestimate connectivity by 2-3x. This is the step that kills most naive placements.

Section 6

Site Selection

DEM local-maxima extraction (5x5 kernel, >150 m floor, 50 m minimum separation) yields 317 true ridge peaks. Those are scored, and a greedy set-cover adds nodes one at a time: round 1 picks maximum initial visibility, subsequent rounds maximize new buildings covered while requiring a clear LoS link back to the existing mesh and a 2.5 km spacing floor.

Section 7

Coverage

Thirty nodes at 12 m AGL produce a combined viewshed of ~547 km^2 (1,440-ray per-node sampling with 4/3-Earth). That reaches 86.7% of Wood County buildings under set-cover and 82.7% under strict per-node LoS - a realistic floor, since real antennas gain from rooftop masts and the 60% Fresnel bar is conservative.

Section 8

The Mesh Graph

Each node links to its four nearest LoS-verified neighbors. 246 pairs close cleanly; paths that fail Fresnel are drawn red. The result is a non-planar graph with triangulated redundancy - any single node or single link failure still leaves a connected subgraph covering the majority of demand.

Section 9

Gateways & Application

Three nodes with the highest direct building LoS are designated gateways - points where a volunteer supplies internet backhaul (MQTT bridge, Starlink, residential fiber) so the mesh can reach outside Wood County when some infrastructure survives. Target applications: EOC→fire-station status pings, SAR team position reports, shelter capacity updates, amateur-radio position beacons.

Section 10

Deployment

Roughly $150 per node in hardware (Heltec/RAK LoRa32, 5.8 dBi collinear, 12 m push-up or existing tower). Power draw under 1 W allows solar + LiFePO₄ at every site. Stage in three phases: gateways first (uplink proves out), Parkersburg-Vienna corridor relays second, rural ridges last. The plan converges on resilient county-wide text messaging for under $5,000 in hardware.
